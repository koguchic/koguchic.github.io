I want to train classic RL and world-model algorithms locally on Atari, and before writing a
line of code I wanted the *map* of the field — not a pile of links, but a route. This is that

## Why Atari, and why the 100k regime

The classic Atari benchmark runs agents for **200 million** frames. The sample-efficient
variant, **Atari-100k**, gives the agent only 100,000 steps — about two hours of play. That
500× difference is what makes this tractable on a laptop, and it's where world models earn their
keep.

Everything is scored by the **human-normalized score**, which places an agent between random
play and a human expert:

$$
\text{HNS} = \frac{s_{\text{agent}} - s_{\text{random}}}{s_{\text{human}} - s_{\text{random}}}
$$

so $\text{HNS} = 1$ means human-level on that game. The quantity every algorithm below is
ultimately trying to maximize is the expected discounted return,

$$
G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k\, r_{t+k}, \qquad \gamma \in [0, 1),
$$

and the entire value-based tradition (Track 01) is built on approximating its expectation with a
neural network $Q_\theta(s, a) \approx \mathbb{E}[G_t \mid s_t = s,\, a_t = a]$.

## Where I think the gap is

Track 07 is the one I keep circling. The dominant Atari-100k world models (Dreamer, IRIS,
DIAMOND) are all **reconstructive** — they predict pixels or image tokens. JEPA's thesis is that
you should predict in *latent* space instead. That idea is well-proven for representation
learning and robotics, but its application to Atari RL is still wide open. *"Does a proper JEPA
world model beat reconstruction-based ones on Atari-100k?"* is not yet settled — which is exactly
the kind of question a laptop-scale benchmark lets you actually chase.

Most of what we believe about machine learning methods, we believe on faith. We run a
world model on Atari, it scores well, and we tell a story about why it worked. The
trouble is that we can't check the story, because on a hard problem we don't know the
right answer. We only know the score.

Tic-Tac-Toe is the opposite kind of problem. It is small enough that we can compute
the exact value of every position and the perfect move in every situation, by hand, in
a few lines of code. That makes it a strange but honest laboratory. Whatever a method
does here, we can hold its output up against the truth and see precisely where it
agrees and where it doesn't.

So I did something that sounds a little absurd on purpose. I took a world model, and a
particular modern kind called a JEPA, and I pointed it at Tic-Tac-Toe. Not because it
is a good way to play the game. It obviously isn't. I did it because when the answer is
knowable, you can watch exactly what the method is and isn't doing, and that turns out
to teach you more than another leaderboard number ever could. This is what I found,
built from scratch and checked at every step against an exact solver.

## A game we can actually check

Let me set up the world plainly, because everything later depends on it.

We play X and move first. After each of our moves, an opponent replies. Our opponent is
deliberately weak: if we are one move from winning it blocks us, and otherwise it plays
at random. It never builds its own threats. The only reward comes at the very end of a
game: $+1$ if we win, $0$ for a draw, $-1$ if we lose. Nothing in between.

That reward structure is the whole difficulty. We are never told that the center is
good, or that corners are strong, or what a fork is. We only find out, at the end,
whether the sequence of moves worked. The agent has to figure out which early moves
deserve credit for a win that arrives five moves later. And the one idea it needs to
discover is the **fork**: a move that creates two threats at once. Our opponent can
only block one of them, so a fork is a guaranteed win. Hold onto that word. It comes
back as the hinge of the entire piece.

Because we know how the opponent behaves, we can skip learning entirely and just
compute the answer. The value of a position is the reward we are about to collect plus
the value of wherever we land next, and if we assume we always play our best move from
here on, that sentence becomes a recursion:

$$ Q^*(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_{s'\sim P(\cdot\mid s,a)}\Big[\, r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s',a') \,\Big], \qquad V^*(s) = \max_a Q^*(s,a). $$

Here $Q^*(s,a)$ is the value of playing move $a$ in position $s$ and then playing
perfectly, and $V^*(s)$ is the value of the best move available. This is the Bellman
equation, and for Tic-Tac-Toe you can evaluate it exactly: every move fills a square,
so positions never repeat, and the recursion bottoms out. It tells us that the first
player, against this opponent, can win about 93% of the time.

That gives us a bar to clear. Two of them, really. The exact solver is the ceiling. And
ordinary tabular Q-learning, which learns a value for each position from experience
rather than computing it, gets essentially all the way there on its own.

Sit with how modest that machinery is. Tabular Q-learning stores a single number for
each of the roughly 1,590 positions it ever sees. No neural network, no gradients. It
learns to win 92% of the time and to never lose, from nothing but the final score. If
you want to beat this opponent, you are already done. Everything that follows is us
asking a different question: not "can we win," but "what does a world model actually do
when we make it try."

## What a world model is, and what a JEPA adds

Q-learning is what people call **model-free**. It never learns how the world works. It
only learns, for each position, how good each move turned out to be. It is pure
bookkeeping over experience.

A **world model** takes the other road. Instead of memorizing outcomes, it tries to
learn the dynamics of the world itself: given the current situation and an action, what
happens next. Once you have that, you can plan. You can imagine playing a move and see
where it leads, without actually playing it. This is the model-based idea, and it is
why world models are appealing on hard problems: a good simulator you carry in your head
lets you think ahead.

A **JEPA**, short for Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture, is a specific way to
build that simulator, and its one distinctive commitment is worth understanding because
it is the reason I chose it. A naive world model predicts the next raw observation: the
next frame of pixels, the next board. That forces it to get every irrelevant detail
right, every leaf and shadow and texture. A JEPA refuses to do that. It first encodes
each observation into an abstract summary, a vector we will call the latent, and then it
predicts the *next latent* rather than the next observation. For a real transition from
state $s$ to state $s'$ under action $a$, it minimizes

$$ \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\big\lVert\, \mathrm{pred}(\mathrm{enc}(s), a) - \mathrm{enc}_{\text{tgt}}(s') \,\big\rVert^2}_{\text{predict the next latent}} \;+\; \lambda\, \underbrace{\mathcal{V}(\mathrm{enc}(s))}_{\text{keep the code alive}}. $$

The point of predicting in latent space is that the encoder is free to throw away
anything not worth predicting. If a detail is unpredictable noise, the encoder learns to
drop it, because keeping it only makes the prediction loss worse. On messy, high-
dimensional inputs this is a real advantage. It is how you turn a wall of pixels into a
compact, useful state.

And here is the tension that makes Tic-Tac-Toe such a revealing test. A JEPA's whole
gift is discarding junk from a messy observation. But the Tic-Tac-Toe board has no junk.
Nine cells, each empty, X, or O. That already *is* the state, exact and minimal, with
nothing to abstract away. So we are running a method precisely in the regime where its
main advantage is switched off, which means whatever value it shows must come from
somewhere else. Watching where that value does and doesn't appear is the experiment.

I built all of it in plain numpy: a small automatic-differentiation engine, the encoder
and predictor networks, and the training loop. Nothing below is hidden inside a library
I can wave at.

## Does it learn the dynamics? Yes.

Start with the most basic question. Can the world model actually predict what happens
next? We train the encoder and predictor on real games, then test them on positions
they were never trained on, and ask how close the predicted next latent is to the true
one. It lands at $R^2 = 0.88$. We can also read off, from the imagined next latent alone,
whether the game is about to end, and it gets that right 85% of the time against a 76%
base rate. The simulator works. The machinery is sound. That matters, because it means
anything that goes wrong later is not the model failing to learn. It is something else.

There is one wrinkle worth pausing on, because it is a failure mode Q-learning simply
cannot have. Look again at the loss. It rewards the predictor for guessing the next
latent correctly. But there is a cheat: if the encoder maps *every* board to the same
point, then the next latent is always that same point, and predicting it is trivial. The
loss goes to zero and the model has learned nothing at all. This is called
representation collapse, and the extra term $\mathcal{V}$ in the loss exists only to
prevent it, by insisting the codes keep some spread. It is easiest to understand by
watching it. Turn the anti-collapse term off and see what happens.

<CollapseToggle client:visible />

Notice the trap. With the regularizer off, the prediction loss reads *better* than with
it on, because a constant is easy to predict, while the representation underneath has
quietly died and become useless for anything. A loss curve that looks great as the model
learns nothing. There is no analog of this in a lookup table. When you can store the
true answer, it cannot silently rot.

## Trying to play with it, and a failure worth understanding

The world model can imagine the game. Now we want it to *play*. The natural move is to
freeze the learned encoder, feed each position through it to get a latent, and put a
small controller on top that reads the latent and picks a move.

My first attempt used the simplest possible controller: a linear one. It reads the
latent, computes a score for each move as a weighted sum, and plays the highest. It was
a disaster. It won 18% of games, worse than where it started. If we stopped here, the
obvious conclusion would be that the world model is worthless.

That conclusion would be wrong, and understanding why is the most useful thing in this
whole piece. The failure was not in the world model. It was in how we read from it.

Think about what it takes to spot a fork. After you play a move, a fork means two
different lines are each one step from winning, at the same time. That is a statement
about a *combination* of conditions being true together. A linear controller cannot
express "these two things hold simultaneously." A weighted sum of the cells can notice
that one line is dangerous, but it cannot carve out the region of positions where two
specific lines are both threats, because that is not a straight-line boundary through
the space. It is the same reason a single linear unit cannot compute XOR. Detecting a
fork is exactly the kind of "two things true together" that a linear model is blind to,
and forks are the only way to win against this opponent.

The fix, then, is not a better world model. It is a controller that can combine
features. Give it one hidden layer and it can build intermediate detectors ("this line
is a threat," "that line is a threat") and then react to their conjunction. Watch what
happens as we grow the controller on top of the exact same frozen latent.

<CapacitySweep client:visible />

A linear head draws or loses. One hidden layer of moderate width climbs to 92%, dead
level with the tabular table. Nothing about the world model changed between those two
points. The only thing that changed was the controller's ability to represent a fork.
The lesson generalizes past this game: when a system built on a learned representation
underperforms, the representation is often fine and the readout is the bottleneck. It is
worth checking which one is actually failing before blaming either.

## The deflating part: you never needed the world model

So a JEPA latent plus a nonlinear controller plays optimally. Before we celebrate, we
have to run the control experiment that the scientific method demands. What happens if
we throw the world model away entirely and put the same nonlinear controller directly on
the raw nine-cell board?

They tie. A plain network reading nine integers plays just as well as the same network
reading a carefully trained latent. This is the honest verdict on the world model as a
way to play Tic-Tac-Toe: at its best it matches what you get for free, and the reason
traces straight back to the tension we noted at the start. A learned representation earns
its keep by turning a bad observation into a good state. The nine-cell board was already
a good state. There was nothing for the encoder to add.

I should mention one more cost before moving on, because it is real and it surprised me.
When you replace the exact lookup table with any learned function of features and keep
updating it by bootstrapping off its own estimates, you lose a guarantee. The table
always converges. The function approximator can diverge, its weights running off toward
infinity, a phenomenon known as the deadly triad. It is not a hypothetical here. With a
normal learning rate it happens.

## Maybe the payoff shows up when data is scarce

At this point a reasonable person pushes back, and I did too. Of course the world model
looks pointless when we can play unlimited games for free. Its real gift is supposed to
be sample efficiency. You learn the dynamics once from cheap, unlabeled experience, and
then you need only a handful of reward-labeled games to learn to act. So I built that
regime directly. Pretrain the encoder on all the game transitions with no rewards at
all, freeze it, and then let the controller learn from only a small number $K$ of
positions where we reveal the correct values.

If the pretrained latent were buying us anything, it would need far fewer labeled
positions than a network learning from scratch. It doesn't.

| labeled positions $K$ | plain network, from scratch | on the pretrained latent |
|---:|:---:|:---:|
| 40 | 46% win | 29% win |
| 160 | 9% win | 24% win |

| everything | 94% win | 92% win |

The two columns wander around inside the noise and end up in the same place. Pretraining
did not save labels, for the same reason it did not help before. When the raw
observation is already a perfect state, there is no better representation waiting to be
discovered by staring at more of it.

So I tried to *manufacture* the conditions a JEPA is actually built for. I made the
observation messy on purpose. Instead of the clean board, the agent now sees a
64-number vector with the board smeared into it and a hundred extra dimensions of pure
noise, re-randomized every time it looks. This is the setting where predicting the next
latent should pay off, because the board is predictable and the noise is not, so a good
encoder should learn to keep the board and discard the noise. A denoiser, learned for
free from unlabeled looks.

<div className="tablewrap">

| labeled positions $K$ | plain network on the noisy view | JEPA-denoised latent |
|---:|:---:|:---:|
| 40 | 25% win | 24% win |
| 160 | 32% win | 31% win |
| everything | 33% win | 35% win |

</div>

A tie again, and now both are stuck around 33%. Two things went wrong, and both are
instructive. First, my noisy view squeezed 127 numbers of board-plus-noise down into 64,
which loses information, so no method can recover the board from a single look and no
method can play well. Second, the way I mixed in the noise was linear, and a plain
network learns to undo a linear mixing on its own, without any pretraining, so there was
no gap left for the world model to fill. To actually see a JEPA win, you would need an
observation that is lossless, scrambled in a genuinely nonlinear way, and hard enough to
decode that unlabeled practice is what teaches you to see. That is a description of a
real perception problem. Which is the quiet point of this whole section: to make the
world model matter, you have to stop doing Tic-Tac-Toe.

## The symmetry you keep hoping it will learn

There is one more hope, and it is the one I held longest, because it feels like it
*should* be true. Tic-Tac-Toe is drenched in symmetry. Rotate the board, reflect it, and
it is the same game. In fact its 1,590 positions are only 218 genuinely different ones
once you account for rotations and reflections. Everything else is a copy at a different
angle. The tabular table is blind to this. It stores all 1,590 as if they were
unrelated. Surely a learned encoder would notice, fold the copies together, and
generalize across them?

To answer this cleanly we have to be careful about a distinction that is easy to blur,
so let me build it from the ground up. Take a real position and its exact best move, and
start rotating.

<SymmetryExplorer client:visible />

Watch the two readouts as you rotate. The *value* never changes. Every rotation of a
winning position is equally winning, worth $+1$. We say the value is **invariant** to the
symmetry. But the *best move* does change. It rotates along with the board, because the
winning square is a physical place, and when the board turns, that place turns with it.
We say the policy is **equivariant**.

That distinction is the whole answer. When I measured the plain JEPA's latent, symmetric
positions were no closer together than random ones. It had not discovered the symmetry.
Feeding it all eight rotations of every position as extra data barely moved the needle.
And when I forced the issue, adding a term to the loss that explicitly pulls the code of
a position toward the codes of its rotations, the symmetry appeared perfectly, every
rotation collapsing onto one point. But the play got *worse*. Of course it did. I had
made the encoder invariant, which throws away orientation, and orientation is exactly
what the policy needs to name which square to play. I had forced the wrong symmetry.

So what does the right one look like? Instead of demanding that a position and its
rotation land on the same point, we can demand that they land in a fixed, orderly
relationship. Pick a matrix $M_R$ for each rotation $R$ and ask the encoder to satisfy
$\mathrm{enc}(Rs) = M_R\,\mathrm{enc}(s)$. Now the latent is not allowed to forget which
way the board is facing. It has to store the orientation in a form that rotates
predictably, like a compass needle that turns as the board turns. This is what
equivariance means as a loss, and it is the symmetry the policy wanted all along.

The nice thing about a problem this small is that we can train all three encoders, the
plain one and the two with symmetry terms, and then look directly at the latent space
each one learned. Below is exactly that. Every dot is one of the 1,590 positions, its
code projected down to three dimensions so we can see it. Drag to turn the cloud, and
switch between the three losses. The highlighted points are one board together with its
eight rotations and reflections.

<LatentOrbit client:visible />

The three pictures are the argument in one place. With no symmetry term, the eight
rotations scatter to unrelated corners of the space; the encoder never learned they were
the same game. The invariance loss crushes all eight onto a single point, which is
precisely why it destroyed play: a single point cannot tell you which way to move. The
equivariance loss spreads them back out, but now onto a clean, regular orbit. The
rotation that turns the board turns its code by a fixed amount, so every rotation is at
once obviously related to the others and still distinct. That is what a usable symmetry
looks like inside a network. And notice what it took to get there. We had to hand the
encoder the group of rotations, as a set of matrices in the loss. It did not discover
the structure on its own, because nothing in "predict the next latent" ever asked it to.

Step back from the details and the real lesson is general, and it is bigger than any one
architecture:

> A network learns to generalize only along the lines you build into it, reward it for,
> or show it in data. It does not invent a symmetry that its objective has no reason to
> want.

Neither "predict the next latent" nor "maximize your score" gets one bit easier if the
encoder folds rotations together. Both objectives are perfectly satisfiable treating
every rotation as its own separate thing. So there is no pressure pushing the network
toward the symmetry, and it never arrives. This is not a flaw in JEPA. It is how
learning works. Convolutional networks did not discover that images are
translation-symmetric; that fact was wired into them by hand, through weight sharing.
Rotation-equivariant networks bake in rotation on purpose. Whenever a model exploits a
symmetry, someone put that symmetry there, in the architecture, the loss, or the data.
You do not get it for free by hoping.

## When a world model is actually worth it

Pulling it together. On Tic-Tac-Toe, a JEPA world model is not the way to play. At its
best it ties a table of numbers you could print on a page, and it charges you an encoder,
a predictor, a delicate anti-collapse term, and a controller for the privilege, plus new
ways to fail that the table never had. Its signature strength, cleaning up a messy
observation, is dead weight when the observation is already clean.

But as a way to *understand*, it was worth every minute, precisely because the answer was
knowable. We got to watch a simulator learn real dynamics, watch a representation die
under a loss that looked healthy, watch a value function diverge, and watch, in slow
motion, the difference between a symmetry of value and a symmetry of action. A lookup
table is too simple to ever show you any of that.

And the exercise draws a clean map of when the machinery earns its keep. A world model
pays off when the observation is a mess you have to see through, when the dynamics are
unknown, when reward is expensive, and when horizons are long enough that planning ahead
matters. Cameras, robots, video. Tic-Tac-Toe has none of those properties, which is
exactly why it makes such a clear window into what the method is doing when its usual
excuses are stripped away. Know the rules? Solve them directly. Don't know them, but the
state is handed to you? Reach for Q-learning. Save the world model for the day your state
arrives as a wall of pixels rather than nine plain integers.

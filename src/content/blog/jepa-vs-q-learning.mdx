Most claims about deep RL methods can't be checked, because the benchmarks are too
big to solve exactly. Tic-Tac-Toe can be solved exactly. So I pointed a JEPA world
model at it, which is obviously overkill, and that's the point: here, every step of
the machinery can be graded against the true answer. Everything below was built from
scratch in numpy and checked against an exact solver.

The plan: understand the opponent, solve the game exactly, then make three learners
try. A Q-table, an MLP, and a JEPA. We'll see who needed what.

## The Opponent, and How to Exploit It

We play X and move first. The reward comes only at the end: $+1$ win, $0$ draw, $-1$
loss. The opponent follows one rule:

> If X is one move from winning, block it. Otherwise, play a random empty cell.

It never builds threats of its own. And a one-threat blocker has exactly one hole:
make **two** threats at once, a *fork*, and it can only block one of them.

<ForkDemo client:visible />

That's the entire game. Every method in this essay is just a different way of
discovering the fork. Before watching them learn it, try the exploit yourself.
The widget also lets you play the two learned agents this essay builds; they play
X and move first, and they are the real trained models, exported move for move.

## Why Q-Learning Solves It: One Number per Move

The value of a move is what you collect now plus the value of where you land,
assuming you keep playing well. Written as equations, the Bellman equations, that's
all it is:

$$
Q^*(s,a) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{s'}\big[\, r \;+\; V^*(s') \,\big]
$$

$$
V^*(s) \;=\; \max_a \, Q^*(s,a)
$$

(No discount factor needed, since every game ends within 9 moves.)

Since we know the opponent's rule, this recursion can be evaluated *exactly*: moves
only fill cells, so positions never repeat, and the recursion bottoms out at the end
of the game. Value flows backward from wins and losses into every earlier move.
Here is what those numbers look like at the moment before the fork:

**Q-learning** is what you do when you *don't* know the opponent's rule: store one
number per (position, move), play games, and after each step nudge the stored number
toward $r + \max_{a'} Q(s', a')$, the right side of the Bellman equation, sampled
from experience instead of computed. For a table this provably converges. On this
game it stores about 1,590 rows, and from nothing but final scores it reaches 92%
wins and never loses. The problem is, honestly, solved. Everything past this point
is curiosity.

## Will a Plain MLP Solve It?

Yes, with one catch that explains half of this essay. Spotting a fork means noticing
that *two* line-threats are true *at the same time*. That's a conjunction, an
XOR-like feature, and a **linear** model provably cannot represent it. A linear
policy on the raw board wins 21% of games and loses 78%. Add one hidden layer and
the same setup wins **93%**, matching the Q-table with just nine input numbers and a
bit of nonlinearity.

Hold onto that lesson: *when a learned system fails, check whether the readout,
rather than the representation, is what can't express the answer.* We're about to
use it.

## Will a JEPA World Model Solve It?

A **world model** learns how the world evolves: given a state and an action, predict
what comes next, so the agent can plan in imagination instead of memorizing
outcomes. A **JEPA** (Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture) is a world model with
one distinctive commitment: it never predicts raw observations. It encodes each
observation into a latent vector and predicts the *next latent*:

$$
\mathcal{L} \;=\; \big\lVert\, \mathrm{pred}(\mathrm{enc}(s),\,a) \;-\; \mathrm{enc}(s') \,\big\rVert^2 \;+\; \lambda\,\mathcal{V}\!\big(\mathrm{enc}(s)\big)
$$

Predicting in latent space lets the encoder throw away unpredictable junk: pixels,
shadows, noise. That's the pitch. Note the irony, though. The Tic-Tac-Toe board has
no junk. Nine cells *is* the perfect minimal state. We are running the method
exactly where its main advantage is switched off, which is what makes the test
clean.

Does it learn the dynamics? Yes: on held-out positions the predicted next latent
matches the true one with $R^2 = 0.88$, and you can read "the game is about to end"
off the imagined latent at 85% accuracy (76% base rate). The simulator works.

The extra term $\mathcal{V}$ guards against JEPA's signature failure: if the encoder
maps *every* board to the same point, the prediction loss hits zero and the model
has learned nothing. This is **representation collapse**, and the nastiest thing
about it is that the loss curve looks *great* while it happens:

Now make it play. Freeze the encoder, put a controller on the latent, and pick
moves:

The linear controller loses, and not because the world model is bad: a linear
readout can't represent a fork. That is the MLP lesson again. Give the controller
one hidden layer and it climbs to **92%**, dead even with the Q-table. And the same
nonlinear controller reading the *raw board* also gets about 93%. The world model
added nothing, because there was nothing for the encoder to clean up.

One hope remains: sample efficiency. Pretrain the encoder for free on unlabeled
play, and maybe you need far fewer reward-labeled games. Measured:

<SampleEfficiency client:visible />

Null result, twice. So: **a JEPA can solve Tic-Tac-Toe, but the world model part of
it is dead weight here.** Everything it earns, a bare MLP earns without it.

## Does JEPA Discover the Board's Symmetry?

This is the hope I held longest. Tic-Tac-Toe's 1,590 positions are only **218**
genuinely different games; everything else is a rotation or reflection. The Q-table
stupidly stores all 1,590. Surely a learned encoder notices that a board and its
rotation are the same game, and folds them together?

**It does not.** In the plain JEPA latent, symmetric boards end up no closer
together than random pairs (within-orbit spread 0.86, versus 0.85 for the raw board:
nothing). Feeding it all 8 rotations of every position as extra data barely moves
the needle.

And it *shouldn't* discover it. That's the real lesson. The training objective is
"predict the next latent," and that objective is perfectly satisfiable with every
orientation living in its own private corner of latent space: rotated board in,
rotated prediction out, loss zero, done. Merging rotations makes the loss no better.
No pressure, no discovery. Networks generalize along the lines you build into the
architecture, reward in the loss, or show in the data, never along lines you merely
hope for. CNNs didn't *discover* that images are translation-symmetric; weight
sharing hard-wired it.

## What Would It Take?

You have to put the symmetry in by hand, and there are two ways to do it, one of
which is a trap. The trap is subtle, so look at the game first:

<SymmetryExplorer client:visible />

The *value* of a position doesn't change when you rotate it: value is **invariant**.
But the *best move* rotates with the board: the policy is **equivariant**. Two
different symmetries, and you must pick the right one.

- **Invariance loss**: pull $\mathrm{enc}(Rs)$ onto $\mathrm{enc}(s)$. Works
  instantly, collapsing the 8 rotations to one point (spread 0.86 → 0.016). And play
  gets *worse*, because the encoder can no longer say which way the board is facing,
  and "which way" is exactly what a move is.
- **Equivariance loss**: demand $\mathrm{enc}(Rs) = M_R\,\mathrm{enc}(s)$ for a
  fixed rotation matrix per symmetry. Now the latent must carry orientation, but in
  a shared, predictable form, like a compass needle that turns with the board.

You can see all three latent geometries directly. This is the whole argument in one
picture:

<LatentOrbit client:visible />

> A network learns to generalize only along the lines you build into it, reward it
> for, or show it in data. It does not invent a symmetry its objective has no reason
> to want.

The JEPA ties. It never wins. It costs an encoder, a predictor, an anti-collapse
regularizer, and a controller, plus failure modes a lookup table cannot have, all to
match what nine integers and one hidden layer give you for free. Its one real
strength, seeing through messy observations, had nothing to do here.

So the map is simple. Know the rules? Solve them with one Bellman line. Don't know
the rules, but the state is clean? Q-learning, or a small MLP. Save the world model
for when the state arrives as a wall of pixels. That is the one job it was built
for, and the one job this game refuses to give it.

One last experiment you can run by hand: scroll back up to the playable board and
face the learned agents, but refuse to act like the blocker. Attack instead.
Neither agent has ever seen an opponent who builds threats. The Q-table walks off
its map within a few moves (the ⚠ marks), and the JEPA answers confidently
everywhere, whether or not its answers are still any good. Distribution shift is
usually an abstraction. Here you can shake its hand.

But as a *microscope*, the exercise paid for itself. We watched a representation die
under a perfect-looking loss, watched a readout get blamed for a representation's
sins (and vice versa), and watched the difference between the symmetry of a value
and the symmetry of an action. These are things you can only *see* on a problem
small enough to have an answer key.
